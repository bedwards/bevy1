# Linear Algebra for Games: The Mathematics of Virtual Worlds

If you want to place an object in a virtual world, move it, rotate it, scale it, or determine whether it can see another object, you need linear algebra. This is not optional, and it is not something you can entirely delegate to the engine, because the engine provides the tools (vectors, matrices, quaternions) and the operations (transformation, projection, interpolation), but deciding which operations to apply, in what order, and for what purpose is the game developer's job. The good news is that the subset of linear algebra used in game development is small, concrete, and deeply geometric: it is about positions, directions, rotations, and the relationships between them, not about abstract vector spaces and eigenvalues. You do not need a mathematics degree to understand it, but you do need to build an intuition for what vectors and transformations mean in the context of a game world, and that intuition comes from practice rather than from reading definitions.

A vector, in game development, is an ordered list of numbers that represents either a position or a direction. A two-dimensional vector has two components, conventionally called x and y, and represents a point or direction in a flat plane. A three-dimensional vector has three components — x, y, and z — and represents a point or direction in space. The distinction between a position and a direction matters conceptually even though they are represented by the same data type: a position says "this is where something is," while a direction says "this is which way something is facing" or "this is how something is moving." A position vector (3, 5, 0) means "three units to the right, five units up, zero units forward." A direction vector (1, 0, 0) means "pointing to the right." Bevy uses the glam library for its vector types, providing Vec2 for 2D and Vec3 for 3D, and the Transform component that every visible entity has includes a translation field (a Vec3 representing position), a rotation field (a quaternion, which we will discuss shortly), and a scale field (a Vec3 representing size along each axis).

The fundamental operations on vectors are addition, subtraction, scalar multiplication, and the two products: dot and cross. Adding two vectors is straightforward component-wise addition: (1, 2) + (3, 4) = (4, 6). Geometrically, adding a direction vector to a position vector moves the position in that direction — this is how movement works in a game. Subtracting one position from another gives the direction from the first to the second, which is how you calculate the path between two entities, the direction a projectile should travel, or whether an enemy is to the left or right of the player. Scalar multiplication scales a vector by a number: multiplying (1, 0, 0) by 5 gives (5, 0, 0), a vector pointing in the same direction but five times longer. This is how you control speed: the velocity is a direction multiplied by a speed value, and the position change per frame is the velocity multiplied by delta time.

The dot product of two vectors produces a single number that encodes the geometric relationship between them. If both vectors are normalized (scaled to length 1), the dot product is the cosine of the angle between them: 1 if they point in the same direction, 0 if they are perpendicular, and -1 if they point in opposite directions. This makes the dot product extraordinarily useful for determining whether two entities are facing each other, whether something is in front of or behind the camera, and how much of a light hits a surface at a given angle. It appears constantly in game code, often in places where you might not expect it, because so many game problems reduce to "what is the angular relationship between these two directions?"

The cross product, which exists only for three-dimensional vectors, produces a new vector that is perpendicular to both input vectors. If you cross the "right" direction with the "forward" direction, you get the "up" direction. This is how coordinate systems are constructed and how surface normals are calculated — the normal of a triangle, which tells lighting systems which way the surface faces, is the cross product of two of its edges. The cross product appears less frequently in game logic than the dot product but is essential in rendering, physics, and any situation where you need to find a direction perpendicular to two other directions.

Matrices are the tool for combining multiple transformations — translation, rotation, and scaling — into a single operation. A 4x4 matrix can represent any combination of moving, rotating, and scaling an object in three-dimensional space, and applying the matrix to a vector transforms that vector according to the combined operation. The reason matrices matter is efficiency: if you need to transform thousands of vertices (as you do when rendering a 3D model), applying a single matrix multiplication is faster than applying a translation, then a rotation, then a scaling separately. Bevy handles matrix construction and application internally in its transform propagation system, so you rarely need to work with matrices directly, but understanding that they exist and what they do helps you understand why Transform has translation, rotation, and scale as separate fields (they are decomposed for ease of use) and why GlobalTransform exists alongside Transform (it is the composed matrix that includes all parent transforms in the hierarchy).

Quaternions are the mechanism Bevy uses for rotation, and they deserve a somewhat extended discussion because they are the one part of game math that most people find genuinely confusing. The alternative to quaternions for representing rotation is Euler angles — three numbers representing rotation around the x, y, and z axes — which are intuitive but suffer from a catastrophic flaw called gimbal lock, where two axes of rotation become aligned and one degree of freedom is lost. Imagine pointing a telescope straight up: you can no longer pan left or right, because the "pan" and "tilt" axes are now aligned. Gimbal lock causes sudden, jarring rotation artifacts in games when objects rotate near the poles of the Euler angle system, and it makes interpolation between rotations (for smooth camera movement or animation blending) unreliable.

Quaternions solve gimbal lock by representing rotation as a four-dimensional number (four components: x, y, z, w) that encodes both an axis of rotation and an angle. You do not need to understand the mathematical derivation of quaternions to use them effectively — what you need to know is that they can represent any rotation without gimbal lock, they can be interpolated smoothly (using spherical linear interpolation, commonly called "slerp"), and they can be composed by multiplication. In Bevy, you create a quaternion from an axis and angle (`Quat::from_axis_angle(Vec3::Y, angle)`), from Euler angles (`Quat::from_euler(EulerRot::XYZ, pitch, yaw, roll)`), or from a rotation that looks from one direction to another (`Transform::looking_at`). Once you have a quaternion, you can apply it to a vector to rotate that vector, multiply two quaternions to combine their rotations, or interpolate between two quaternions to smoothly transition from one orientation to another.

The Transform component in Bevy ties all of this together. Every entity that has a visual presence in the world — every sprite, every mesh, every camera, every light — has a Transform that specifies where it is (translation), which way it faces (rotation, as a quaternion), and how big it is (scale). The GlobalTransform component, which is automatically computed by Bevy's transform propagation system, represents the entity's final position in the world after accounting for all parent transforms in the hierarchy. If an entity is a child of another entity, its Transform is relative to its parent: a sword with translation (1, 0, 0) that is a child of a character at position (5, 0, 0) has a global position of (6, 0, 0). This hierarchical composition of transforms is one of the fundamental reasons for having a parent-child relationship between entities, and it is implemented through matrix multiplication of the parent's GlobalTransform with the child's local Transform.

Interpolation — the process of computing values between two known values — appears everywhere in game development, from movement smoothing to animation blending to color gradients, and it is fundamentally a linear algebra operation. Linear interpolation (lerp) between two values a and b at parameter t (ranging from 0 to 1) gives `a + (b - a) * t`, which is a weighted average: at t=0 you get a, at t=1 you get b, and at t=0.5 you get the midpoint. This works for numbers, vectors, and colors (which are vectors in color space), and it is the building block for more complex interpolation schemes. Spherical linear interpolation (slerp) does the same thing for quaternions, following the shortest arc on a four-dimensional sphere rather than a straight line, which produces visually correct smooth rotation. Easing functions modify the parameter t with a curve (ease in, ease out, ease in-out) to make interpolation feel natural rather than mechanical — objects in the real world do not start and stop moving at constant speed, and applying an easing function to movement makes it feel like the object has mass and momentum.

The beauty of the linear algebra used in games is that it is deeply visual: every operation corresponds to something you can see or imagine. Adding a velocity to a position moves an object. Taking the dot product tells you how much two directions agree. Multiplying by a matrix transforms an object from one space to another. Interpolating between two rotations smoothly turns something from one orientation to another. The math is not abstract — it is the geometry of the virtual world made precise, and developing fluency with it is one of the most rewarding investments a game developer can make, because it provides the vocabulary for expressing ideas about space, movement, and shape that are the raw material of every game you will ever build.
