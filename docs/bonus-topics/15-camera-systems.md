# Camera Systems in Games

Every game, no matter how simple or complex, has a camera. Even a game that draws directly to a fixed-size pixel buffer is making an implicit camera decision: the camera is locked, staring straight down (or straight ahead), and it never moves. The moment you want to scroll a level, follow a character, zoom in on an explosion, or show a cutscene from a dramatic angle, you are doing camera work, and the quality of that camera work has an outsized impact on how your game feels to play. A brilliant game with a bad camera is a frustrating game. A mediocre game with a perfectly tuned camera can feel surprisingly polished. This is because the camera is the player's window into your world, and every frame they see is framed by decisions you made about where the camera sits, what it looks at, and how it moves.

This essay explores camera systems from the ground up, starting with the mathematical foundations of projection, moving through the most common camera behavior patterns used in two-dimensional and three-dimensional games, and arriving at how Bevy implements cameras and the new controller components introduced in recent versions of the engine.

## Projection: How a Camera Sees the World

At the most fundamental level, a camera in a game engine is a mathematical transformation that converts three-dimensional world coordinates into two-dimensional screen coordinates. Even in a two-dimensional game, this transformation exists; it is just simpler. There are two primary types of projection, and understanding them is essential for working with any camera system.

Orthographic projection is the simpler of the two. In an orthographic projection, parallel lines remain parallel after projection. Objects do not get smaller as they move further from the camera. A one-meter cube looks exactly the same size whether it is one meter away or one thousand meters away. This might sound unrealistic, and it is, but it is exactly what you want for many types of games. Most two-dimensional games use orthographic projection because it preserves the spatial relationships that players expect: a tile on the left side of the screen is the same size as a tile on the right side, and scrolling the camera does not cause any distortion. Isometric games, top-down games, side-scrollers, strategy games with a bird's-eye view, and most retro-style games all use orthographic projection. The math is straightforward: you define a rectangular region of the world (the view rectangle), and everything inside that rectangle is mapped linearly to the screen. Moving the camera is just shifting the rectangle. Zooming is just changing the rectangle's size.

Perspective projection is what mimics human vision and real cameras. In a perspective projection, objects further from the camera appear smaller. Parallel lines converge toward a vanishing point on the horizon. This is the projection used by nearly all three-dimensional games that want a sense of depth and immersion: first-person shooters, third-person action games, racing games, flight simulators, and so on. The math is more involved because it requires dividing by the depth coordinate, which creates the foreshortening effect that makes distant objects shrink. The key parameters of a perspective projection are the field of view (the angle of the camera's cone of vision, typically measured vertically), the aspect ratio (the width-to-height ratio of the screen), and the near and far clipping planes (the minimum and maximum distances at which objects are visible). The field of view is particularly important for game feel: a narrow field of view creates a telephoto effect that makes the world feel compressed and claustrophobic, while a wide field of view creates a fisheye effect that exaggerates distances and gives a sense of speed. First-person shooters typically use a field of view around sixty-five to ninety degrees, and many players have strong preferences, which is why field-of-view sliders are a common accessibility feature.

There is also a less common but occasionally useful hybrid called oblique projection, used in some isometric and cabinet-style games, but orthographic and perspective cover the vast majority of cases.

In Bevy, the distinction between these two projection types is captured by the camera components. When you spawn a camera entity with the Camera2d component, you get an orthographic projection suitable for two-dimensional games. When you spawn with Camera3d, you get a perspective projection suitable for three-dimensional games, though you can configure Camera3d to use orthographic projection if you want a three-dimensional scene rendered without perspective foreshortening (a common choice for strategy games and certain artistic styles). The projection parameters are configurable through associated components, and Bevy's defaults are sensible starting points for most games.

## Camera Behaviors: Follow, Smooth, Shake

Projection determines how the camera sees the world, but camera behavior determines where the camera is and how it moves. This is where the real artistry of camera programming lies, and where subtle differences in implementation create dramatically different player experiences.

The simplest camera behavior is the locked camera, where the camera does not move at all. This works for single-screen games like Tetris or many puzzle games. The next simplest is the direct follow, where the camera's position is set to exactly match the player's position every frame. This works, but it feels rigid and mechanical. Every tiny movement the player makes is transmitted directly to the camera, which means that rapid direction changes cause the entire world to jerk back and forth. Players find this disorienting, especially in fast-paced games.

The first refinement is smoothing, also called lerping or easing, where the camera moves toward the player's position gradually rather than snapping to it instantly. Instead of setting the camera position equal to the player position, you move the camera a fraction of the distance toward the player each frame. The result is that the camera gently drifts to center on the player, absorbing small movements without jerking. The smoothing factor controls the feel: a high smoothing factor (moving quickly toward the player) feels responsive but still slightly cushioned, while a low smoothing factor (moving slowly) creates a floaty, cinematic feel where the camera lags behind the player significantly. Most platformers and top-down games use some form of smoothing, and tuning the smoothing factor is one of those "feel" adjustments that can take hours of playtesting to get right.

The dead zone pattern adds another layer of sophistication. A dead zone (sometimes called a dead band or a camera window) is a rectangular region in the center of the screen. As long as the player stays within this region, the camera does not move at all. Only when the player reaches the edge of the dead zone does the camera begin to follow. This is useful because it allows the player to make small movements without the camera reacting, which reduces visual noise and gives the player a sense of agency over what they see. Many classic platformers use dead zones: the player can walk back and forth in a small area without the camera scrolling, and the camera only scrolls when the player pushes past the boundary. The size of the dead zone is a design choice; a large dead zone gives the player more freedom to explore the edges of the screen, while a small dead zone keeps the player more centered.

Look-ahead is a pattern where the camera anticipates the player's movement direction and shifts slightly ahead of them. If the player is running to the right, the camera pans a bit to the right so the player can see more of what is coming. If the player reverses direction, the camera smoothly shifts to show more of the space in the new direction. This is particularly valuable in side-scrolling games where threats come from the direction of movement, and players need to see what they are running into. Implementing look-ahead requires tracking the player's velocity or facing direction and offsetting the camera target accordingly, usually with smoothing applied to the offset so the camera does not jerk when the player changes direction.

Vertical snapping is a pattern specific to platformers where the camera only adjusts its vertical position when the player lands on a platform, not while they are in the air. This prevents the camera from bouncing up and down with every jump, which would be visually exhausting. The camera locks to the player's horizontal movement but waits for a "grounded" state before adjusting vertically. Some games refine this further by only snapping vertically when the player lands on a platform that is significantly above or below the camera's current vertical position, allowing small hops to be absorbed without any camera movement.

Screen shake is not a movement pattern so much as a special effect, but it is camera-related and deserves discussion. Screen shake is achieved by adding a small random offset to the camera's position each frame for a short duration, creating a vibration effect that communicates impact, explosion, or other dramatic events. The key parameters are amplitude (how far the camera moves), frequency (how fast it vibrates), duration (how long the shake lasts), and decay (how the amplitude decreases over time, usually exponentially). A common mistake is making screen shake too strong or too long, which becomes nauseating. Subtlety is key. A few pixels of offset for a quarter of a second is often enough to sell an impact. Screen shake should almost always be an option that players can reduce or disable, as it can cause discomfort or accessibility issues for some players. In Bevy, screen shake is typically implemented by adding a temporary offset to the camera's Transform component, driven by a timer and a random number generator.

Split screen and picture-in-picture are multi-camera patterns used in local multiplayer games or games that need to show multiple viewpoints simultaneously. In a split-screen setup, the screen is divided into two or four regions, each rendered by a separate camera following a different player. In Bevy, this is achieved by spawning multiple camera entities, each with a viewport configuration that specifies which rectangular portion of the screen it renders to. The cameras are independent; each has its own projection, position, and behavior. Picture-in-picture is similar but uses a small overlay camera (a minimap, a rear-view mirror in a racing game, a security camera feed) rendered on top of the main view. Bevy's camera ordering and viewport system make both of these patterns straightforward to implement, though the performance cost of rendering the scene multiple times is something to be aware of.

## Bevy's Camera Components and Controllers

Bevy has evolved its camera system significantly over its development history. In early versions, cameras were somewhat bare-bones: you spawned a camera entity with the appropriate bundle and then wrote your own systems to move it. This was flexible but meant that every project had to reinvent basic camera behaviors like following a target or orbiting around a point.

The Camera2d and Camera3d components remain the foundation of Bevy's camera system. Camera2d sets up an orthographic projection and is the starting point for any two-dimensional game. Camera3d sets up a perspective projection and is the starting point for three-dimensional games. Both can be customized extensively: you can change the projection type, the clear color (what color the screen is filled with before rendering), the rendering order (for multi-camera setups), and the viewport. These components work in conjunction with Bevy's Transform component, so moving the camera is as simple as modifying its transform, just like moving any other entity.

Bevy 0.18 introduced built-in camera controller components that address the most common camera needs without requiring custom systems. The FreeCamera controller provides a first-person-style camera that responds to keyboard and mouse input, allowing the player (or, more commonly, the developer during testing) to fly freely through the scene. This is invaluable during development because it lets you inspect your world from any angle without writing movement code. You simply add the FreeCamera component to your camera entity, and it handles input processing, rotation, and translation automatically. The movement speed, sensitivity, and key bindings are configurable through the component's fields.

The PanCamera controller is designed for top-down and strategy-style games where the camera looks down at the world and the player pans around by moving the mouse to the screen edges, clicking and dragging, or using keyboard keys. It supports zooming in and out, movement bounds (so the camera cannot scroll past the edges of the map), and smooth movement. Like FreeCamera, you add it as a component to your camera entity and it handles the rest, though you can customize its behavior through configuration fields.

These built-in controllers are implemented as regular Bevy systems and components, which means they compose with the rest of your game. If you need to temporarily disable the camera controller during a cutscene, you can remove the component and add it back when the cutscene ends. If you need behavior that the built-in controllers do not support, you can write your own system that modifies the camera's transform, and it will work alongside or instead of the built-in controller. The built-in controllers are conveniences, not constraints.

For games that need more sophisticated camera behavior, such as a third-person camera that orbits the player while avoiding clipping through walls, or a cinematic camera that follows a spline path during a cutscene, you will still need to write custom systems. But the patterns are the same as for any other Bevy system: query for the camera entity's transform, compute the desired position and rotation based on whatever logic you need, and update the transform accordingly, usually with smoothing applied to avoid jarring movement. The camera is just another entity, and camera programming in Bevy is just writing systems that manipulate that entity's components.

One subtle but important detail about camera systems in Bevy is transform propagation. If you make the camera a child of another entity (like the player), the camera's transform will be relative to its parent. This means you can create a third-person camera by making the camera a child of the player entity, offset backward and upward, and the camera will automatically follow the player as the player moves. Rotation requires more care because you probably do not want the camera to inherit all of the player's rotation (you do not want the camera to tilt when the player leans), but for simple cases, the parent-child relationship is an elegant way to implement camera follow without any explicit follow logic at all.

The richness of camera programming is one of those areas where game development reveals itself as a craft as much as a science. The math is not particularly difficult; it is mostly linear interpolation, some trigonometry for rotations, and perhaps a raycast or two for collision avoidance. But the feel, the intangible quality that makes a camera feel "right," comes from iteration, playtesting, and a willingness to spend time on something that players will never consciously notice unless it is wrong. A great camera disappears. The player forgets it exists because it always shows them exactly what they need to see, when they need to see it, without any fuss. That invisibility is the highest compliment a camera system can receive, and achieving it is one of the quiet pleasures of game development.
