# Tilemaps and 2D World Building

There is a reason that so many of the most beloved games in history are built on grids. From the original Super Mario Bros. to Stardew Valley, from Dwarf Fortress to Celeste, the humble tilemap has been the foundation of two-dimensional game worlds for over four decades. The idea is disarmingly simple: divide the world into a regular grid of cells, assign a visual tile to each cell, and compose those tiles into a coherent landscape. What makes this simple idea so enduring is not just nostalgia or aesthetic preference, though both play a role, but the extraordinary practical advantages that tilemaps provide for level design, collision detection, memory efficiency, and the sheer speed at which a developer can build and iterate on a game world.

This essay explores the tilemap concept from its fundamentals through to its modern implementation in Bevy, covering the tradeoffs between tile-based and free-form two-dimensional worlds, the clever technique of autotiling, collision detection against tile geometry, the coordinate systems involved, layering strategies for visual depth, and the parallax scrolling effect that gives flat worlds the illusion of dimension.

## What a Tilemap Is, and Why It Endures

A tilemap is, at its core, a two-dimensional array where each element references a tile from a tileset, which is a collection of small, usually square images designed to fit together seamlessly. The tileset might contain a grass tile, a dirt tile, a water tile, various wall tiles, decorative tiles for flowers or rocks, and so on. The tilemap array says "cell (0,0) is grass, cell (1,0) is dirt, cell (2,0) is water," and the rendering system draws the appropriate tile image at each grid position. A single tileset image might be 256 by 256 pixels containing sixty-four individual 32-by-32-pixel tiles, and a tilemap built from those tiles might be hundreds or thousands of cells in each dimension, representing a world far larger than could fit on screen at once.

The memory efficiency of this approach is remarkable. Instead of storing a unique image for every pixel of a large world, you store one tileset image and a compact array of tile indices. A 1000-by-1000 tilemap with single-byte tile indices takes only about one megabyte of storage, yet it can represent a world of considerable size and visual variety. This efficiency was critical in the early days of gaming when memory was measured in kilobytes, and it remains valuable today because it makes tilemaps trivially fast to load, save, and transmit over a network.

But the advantages of tilemaps go beyond memory. The grid structure itself is a gift to game logic. Collision detection against a grid is vastly simpler than collision detection against arbitrary geometry. To check whether a character is standing on solid ground, you convert their position to tile coordinates (by dividing by the tile size and rounding), look up the tile at that position, and check whether it is solid. There is no need for complex polygon intersection tests or spatial indexing; the grid itself is the spatial index. Pathfinding on a grid is straightforward because the grid naturally defines a graph where each cell is a node and adjacent cells are connected by edges. Level design tools for tilemaps are easy to build because the editing operations are inherently discrete: place a tile, remove a tile, fill a region, paint with a brush. The constraint of the grid paradoxically liberates the level designer by reducing the decision space to manageable dimensions.

The distinction between tile-based and free-form two-dimensional worlds is worth considering. A free-form two-dimensional world, sometimes called a vector-based world, places objects at arbitrary positions with arbitrary sizes and orientations. This gives maximum artistic freedom but requires more complex collision detection, often using physics engines, and makes level design harder because every element must be positioned precisely. Many modern two-dimensional games use a hybrid approach: the world's underlying structure is tile-based for collision and navigation, but the visual presentation includes free-form elements like decorative sprites, particle effects, and characters that move smoothly between tile positions. This gives you the structural advantages of a grid with the visual richness of free-form placement.

## Autotiling and Visual Polish

One of the challenges of tilemap-based worlds is making them look good. A naively constructed tilemap, where you simply stamp down grass and dirt tiles on a grid, tends to look blocky and artificial. The boundaries between different terrain types are sharp, one-tile-wide transitions that scream "I am made of squares." Autotiling is the technique that solves this problem, and understanding it illuminates the surprising depth hidden in apparently simple two-dimensional graphics.

The idea behind autotiling is that the tile drawn in a given cell should depend not just on what terrain type occupies that cell but also on what terrain types occupy its neighbors. A grass cell surrounded entirely by other grass cells should draw a plain grass tile. But a grass cell with dirt to its right should draw a grass tile with a dirt-colored right edge, a transition tile that smoothly blends the two terrain types. A grass cell with dirt to its right and below should draw a corner transition tile. By providing transition tiles for every possible combination of neighbor states and selecting the correct tile automatically based on the neighborhood, you can create smooth, natural-looking terrain boundaries without the level designer having to manually place every transition tile.

The challenge is combinatorial. If you consider only the four cardinal neighbors (up, down, left, right), each of which can be either "same" or "different," you have sixteen possible neighborhood configurations, requiring up to sixteen tiles per terrain type. If you also consider diagonal neighbors, which you need for smooth corners, the number of configurations jumps to two hundred and fifty-six, which is unmanageable. In practice, autotiling systems use various tricks to reduce the number of required tiles. The most common approach is the "blob" or "bitmasking" method, where you assign a bit to each neighbor, compute a bitmask from the neighborhood, and use a lookup table to map bitmasks to tile indices. Careful tileset design can collapse many bitmask values to the same tile, reducing the number of unique tiles needed. A well-designed autotile tileset for a single terrain type might have forty-seven or forty-eight unique tiles, which covers all visually distinct configurations when you account for symmetries and the fact that some configurations are aesthetically equivalent.

Creating autotile tilesets is an art form in itself. The tiles must be designed so that every possible combination of neighbors produces a visually coherent result. The edges of each tile must align perfectly with the edges of adjacent tiles. Transition tiles must smoothly blend their parent terrain types without visible seams. This is painstaking work, but the payoff is enormous: a level designer can paint terrain types in broad strokes, and the autotiling system handles all the visual detail automatically. Modern tilemap editors, including popular tools like Tiled (a free, open-source tilemap editor that Bevy can import from), have built-in autotiling support that makes this workflow smooth and intuitive.

## Collision with Tiles and the Coordinate System

Collision detection in a tile-based world exploits the regularity of the grid to achieve simplicity and speed that free-form collision systems cannot match. The fundamental operation is converting between world coordinates (continuous floating-point positions measured in pixels or meters) and tile coordinates (discrete integer positions measured in tiles). If your tiles are 32 pixels wide and 32 pixels tall, then the tile at world position (100.0, 75.0) is at tile coordinates (3, 2), computed by dividing each world coordinate by the tile size and taking the floor. This conversion is cheap, constant-time arithmetic, no searching required.

To check whether a game entity collides with the tile world, you determine which tiles the entity's bounding box overlaps by converting the bounding box's corners to tile coordinates, then check each overlapped tile to see if it is solid. For a small entity (smaller than a single tile), this involves checking at most four tiles: the four grid cells that the entity's corners might touch. Even for large entities, the number of tiles to check is proportional to the entity's size divided by the tile size, which is usually a small number. Compare this to free-form collision detection, where checking an entity against the world might involve broadphase spatial queries, narrowphase polygon intersection tests, and manifold generation. The grid makes all of this unnecessary for tile-based collision.

Tile collision also simplifies response. When an entity collides with a tile, you know the tile's exact position and extent (it occupies a grid-aligned rectangle of known size), so computing the penetration depth and the direction to push the entity out is straightforward geometry. Many platformers implement collision response by resolving the horizontal and vertical components of movement separately: first move horizontally, check for tile collisions and push back, then move vertically, check for tile collisions and push back. This two-pass approach avoids the corner cases that plague simultaneous collision resolution and works remarkably well in practice.

The tile coordinate system itself deserves attention because there are choices to be made that affect everything downstream. The most common convention is that tile (0, 0) is at the top-left corner of the map, with the x-axis pointing right and the y-axis pointing down. This matches screen coordinates and is intuitive for level designers working in a two-dimensional editor. However, Bevy uses a coordinate system where the y-axis points up, following the mathematical convention and consistent with how Bevy handles three-dimensional space. This means that when working with tilemaps in Bevy, you may need to flip the y-axis when converting between tile coordinates (y-down) and world coordinates (y-up). This is a minor inconvenience but a common source of bugs if you are not aware of it. Bevy's built-in tilemap support handles this conversion for you, but understanding the underlying coordinate mismatch helps you debug issues when they arise.

Bevy introduced built-in tilemap support starting with version 0.17, which was a significant milestone for the engine's two-dimensional game development story. Prior to that, tilemap functionality was provided by community plugins, most notably bevy_ecs_tilemap, which was widely used and well-regarded. The built-in support brings tilemap functionality into the core engine, ensuring that it is maintained alongside the rest of Bevy and benefits from the same performance optimizations and API consistency. The built-in system supports creating tilemaps as entities with tile components, allows you to specify tilesets as texture atlases, and provides efficient rendering that batches tile draw calls for performance. You define a tilemap by specifying its dimensions, tile size, and the data for each cell, and Bevy handles the rendering automatically, including only drawing tiles that are visible within the camera's view (a technique called frustum culling that prevents wasted rendering work on off-screen tiles).

## Layering, Parallax, and the Illusion of Depth

A single layer of tiles creates a flat, gameboard-like world. Real two-dimensional games almost always use multiple layers to create visual richness and the impression of depth. A typical layering setup for a platformer might include a background layer (sky, distant mountains, clouds), a midground layer (the main terrain the player interacts with), a decoration layer (flowers, torches, signs that are rendered in front of the terrain but do not have collision), and a foreground layer (elements that are rendered in front of the player, like overhanging tree branches or cave ceilings). Each layer is a separate tilemap, rendered in order from back to front, with the player entity rendered at the appropriate point in the stacking order.

Bevy handles layering through its rendering order system. Each tilemap entity has a transform with a z-coordinate, and entities with lower z-values are rendered behind entities with higher z-values. By assigning different z-values to your tilemap layers, you control the visual stacking order. The player entity's z-value determines where in the stack the player appears. If you want the player to walk behind a foreground layer (like passing under a bridge), you set the foreground layer's z-value higher than the player's. If you want the player to walk in front of a background layer, you set the background layer's z-value lower. This z-based ordering is simple and flexible, and it composes naturally with Bevy's existing transform and rendering systems.

Parallax scrolling is the technique that makes layered two-dimensional worlds feel truly alive. The idea comes from a real-world phenomenon: when you look out the window of a moving car, nearby objects (trees by the roadside) appear to move quickly past your field of vision, while distant objects (mountains on the horizon) appear to move slowly or not at all. Parallax scrolling simulates this depth cue by moving different tilemap layers at different speeds relative to the camera. The background layer (distant mountains) scrolls at, say, ten percent of the camera's speed, so it barely moves as the camera pans. The midground layer (the main terrain) scrolls at one hundred percent of the camera's speed, moving in lockstep with the camera. A near-foreground layer might scroll at one hundred and twenty percent, moving slightly faster than the camera to create the impression of being very close to the viewer.

Implementing parallax in Bevy involves writing a system that adjusts each layer's position based on the camera's position and a per-layer parallax factor. When the camera moves by some amount in the x direction, each layer moves by that amount multiplied by its parallax factor. A factor of 1.0 means the layer moves exactly with the camera (no parallax effect). A factor of 0.5 means the layer moves at half the camera's speed (appearing distant). A factor of 0.0 means the layer does not move at all (infinitely distant, like a skybox). A factor greater than 1.0 means the layer moves faster than the camera (appearing very close). The effect is striking: even with completely flat, two-dimensional art, parallax scrolling creates a convincing sense of depth that draws the player into the world.

There is a subtlety to parallax scrolling that catches many developers off guard: when the camera is at the world origin, all layers should be aligned (their decorative features should line up as the artist intended). As the camera moves away from the origin, the layers drift apart according to their parallax factors. This means that the parallax calculation should be relative to some reference point, usually the world origin or the center of the map, not the camera's absolute position. Getting this wrong causes the layers to be misaligned when the game starts or when the player returns to the starting area.

Tilemaps, for all their simplicity, are one of the most rewarding tools in a game developer's kit. They impose just enough structure to make world-building fast and collision detection easy, while leaving ample room for artistic expression through autotiling, layering, and parallax. The grid is a constraint, yes, but it is the kind of constraint that focuses creativity rather than limiting it. Some of the most visually stunning two-dimensional games ever made, from Owlboy to Hyper Light Drifter to Dead Cells, are built on tilemaps at their core, with free-form elements layered on top for additional richness. Bevy's built-in tilemap support makes this fundamental building block accessible within the engine's elegant Entity Component System architecture, and as the feature matures, it will only become more powerful and easier to use. If you are building a two-dimensional game in Bevy, the tilemap should be one of the first tools you reach for, because for all its apparent simplicity, it will carry you further than you might expect.
