# Accessibility in Games

When we talk about accessibility in games, we are talking about something more fundamental than feature checklists or compliance checkboxes. We are talking about who gets to play. Games are one of humanity's oldest cultural forms, older than written language, and the digital games of the past half-century have become one of the most popular and economically significant forms of entertainment on the planet. Billions of people play games. And yet, for a significant fraction of the population, many games are partially or completely inaccessible due to design choices that assume a narrow range of physical and cognitive abilities. According to the World Health Organization, roughly sixteen percent of the global population experiences significant disability. In the United States alone, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimates that one in four adults has some form of disability. These are not edge cases; they represent hundreds of millions of potential players who are excluded from games that were not designed with their needs in mind.

The good news is that accessibility in games has improved dramatically over the past decade, driven by advocacy from disabled gamers, groundbreaking accessible titles that proved the commercial viability of inclusive design, and platform-level initiatives like the Xbox Accessibility Guidelines that have raised the bar for the entire industry. The further good news is that many accessibility features benefit all players, not just those with disabilities, which means investing in accessibility is not charity but sound design.

This essay explores the major categories of accessibility in games, the design patterns and technical implementations that address them, and how game developers, including those working with Bevy, can build more inclusive experiences.

## Categories of Accessibility

Accessibility is not a single problem but a constellation of related challenges, each corresponding to a different type of disability or impairment. The major categories are visual, auditory, motor, and cognitive accessibility, and each encompasses a range of conditions from mild to severe.

Visual accessibility addresses the needs of players who are blind, have low vision, are colorblind, or have other visual impairments. Colorblindness alone affects approximately eight percent of males and half a percent of females of Northern European descent, with different prevalence rates in other populations. The most common form, red-green colorblindness (deuteranopia and protanopia), makes it difficult or impossible to distinguish between red and green, which is a problem when games use these colors to indicate enemies versus allies, health versus damage, safe zones versus danger zones, or success versus failure. Colorblind modes that shift the color palette to use distinguishable colors (often substituting blue and orange for red and green) are one of the most impactful accessibility features a game can offer, and they are relatively straightforward to implement through palette swaps or shader modifications.

Beyond colorblindness, low vision affects a large population of players, particularly older adults. Small text, thin fonts, low-contrast user interfaces, and tiny icons are common barriers. Scalable text, high-contrast modes, and options to increase the size of user interface elements address these issues. Some games offer a "large text" mode; better games make text size a continuous slider that players can adjust to their needs. The key principle is that information should never be conveyed solely through visual detail that some players cannot perceive; there should always be redundant channels (text labels in addition to color coding, shapes in addition to colors, audio cues in addition to visual effects).

For players who are fully blind, the challenges are more profound but not insurmountable. A growing number of games are designed from the ground up for blind players, using audio as the primary channel for spatial information, game state, and player feedback. Audio-based games have a long history, and mainstream games are increasingly incorporating features like screen reader support for menus and user interfaces, spatial audio cues that convey the position of objects and enemies, and narrated descriptions of visual content. The Last of Us Part II was a landmark in this regard, offering a suite of accessibility features so comprehensive that blind players could complete the entire game, a commercial blockbuster, not a niche accessibility project. This demonstrated that accessibility and mainstream commercial success are not in tension.

Auditory accessibility addresses the needs of players who are deaf or hard of hearing. Subtitles are the most basic and widely expected auditory accessibility feature, but there is a world of difference between minimal subtitles (dialogue text displayed at the bottom of the screen) and comprehensive subtitles (speaker identification, directional indicators, descriptions of important non-dialogue sounds like footsteps, gunfire, or alarms, presented in a readable format with customizable size, background opacity, and color). Closed captions, which describe all significant sounds rather than just dialogue, are the gold standard. Beyond subtitles, games can provide visual indicators for sounds that convey gameplay information: a visual ping or flash indicating the direction of an off-screen sound, a visual representation of music intensity to convey mood, or a heads-up display element that shows the direction of nearby sounds. Vibration and haptic feedback through controllers provide another non-auditory channel for conveying timing, impact, and rhythm.

Motor accessibility addresses the needs of players with limited mobility, reduced dexterity, or conditions that affect their ability to use standard input devices. This encompasses a vast range of situations: a player with a spinal cord injury who uses a mouth-operated joystick, a player with cerebral palsy who has difficulty pressing multiple buttons simultaneously, a player with repetitive strain injury who cannot sustain rapid button pressing, or an older player whose reaction time has slowed. The single most important motor accessibility feature is remappable controls, the ability for the player to assign any game action to any input. This seems obvious, yet many games ship with fixed control schemes that cannot be changed, or that can only be changed from a limited set of presets. Full remapping allows players to configure their input devices to match their physical capabilities, whether that means a standard controller, a keyboard, an adaptive controller like the Xbox Adaptive Controller, a flight stick, a set of foot pedals, or any other input device.

Beyond remapping, motor accessibility features include the ability to change from button holds to toggles (so a player does not need to sustain pressure on a button), adjustable timing windows for quicktime events or timed inputs, the option to slow down gameplay, auto-aim or aim assist that reduces the precision required for targeting, and one-handed control schemes that map all essential functions to a subset of inputs. Some games offer a "copilot" mode where two controllers are merged into a single player, allowing a helper to assist with inputs the primary player finds difficult.

Cognitive accessibility is perhaps the broadest and least well-understood category. It encompasses conditions like attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), dyslexia, autism spectrum conditions, intellectual disabilities, traumatic brain injury, and age-related cognitive decline. The design implications are varied: clear and consistent user interface design, the ability to pause and resume at any time (including during cutscenes), objective markers and quest logs that remind players what they are supposed to be doing, tutorials that can be repeated, simplified language options, and the avoidance of sensory overload (flashing lights, overwhelming visual clutter, too many simultaneous audio sources). Content warnings for potentially distressing material (violence, jump scares, flashing patterns that could trigger photosensitive epilepsy) also fall under cognitive accessibility.

## Difficulty Options as Accessibility

One of the most contentious topics in gaming accessibility is the relationship between difficulty and accessibility. Some designers argue that a fixed difficulty level is integral to a game's artistic vision, that the challenge is the experience, and that reducing difficulty undermines the intended design. Others argue that difficulty options are an accessibility feature because they allow players with different abilities, including but not limited to disabilities, to experience the game's content.

The practical reality is that difficulty options benefit an enormous range of players. A player with a motor disability who cannot execute rapid inputs benefits from an easy mode that relaxes timing requirements. A player with a cognitive disability who struggles to track complex systems benefits from a simplified mode. A casual player who wants to experience the story without mastering the combat benefits from a narrative mode. A very skilled player who wants a greater challenge benefits from a hard mode. And a player returning after a long break benefits from being able to lower the difficulty until they re-acclimate.

The most inclusive approach is granular difficulty customization rather than simple easy, medium, and hard presets. Let players independently adjust combat difficulty, puzzle difficulty, navigation assistance, resource abundance, and death penalties. This allows each player to configure the experience to match their specific strengths and limitations, rather than forcing a one-size-fits-all adjustment. The Forza Horizon series has been lauded for this approach, offering dozens of independent assist options that players can mix and match.

The argument that accessibility features "ruin" a game's intended experience is undermined by a simple observation: the features are optional. A player who wants the full, unassisted experience can leave all accessibility features off. Their experience is unchanged. Meanwhile, other players who could not play the game at all can now enjoy it. The cost is development time; the benefit is a larger, more diverse audience and the satisfaction of creating something that more people can enjoy.

## Implementation and the Xbox Accessibility Guidelines

For developers looking for concrete guidance, the Xbox Accessibility Guidelines (XAGs) are the most comprehensive and well-regarded set of accessibility recommendations in the game industry. Published by Microsoft and freely available, the XAGs cover twenty-three categories of accessibility, each with specific, actionable recommendations. They are not Xbox-specific despite the name; the recommendations apply to games on any platform. The guidelines cover text display (minimum sizes, contrast ratios, customizable colors), audio (subtitles, captions, visual alternatives to audio cues), input (remapping, sensitivity adjustment, alternative input support), user interface (screen reader support, navigation patterns, focus indicators), difficulty and assist options, photosensitivity (avoiding dangerous flash patterns, providing warnings), and online safety (communication controls, moderation tools). Each guideline includes a rationale explaining who it helps and why, making the document an excellent educational resource as well as a practical checklist.

Implementing accessibility features in a game engine like Bevy involves both design-level and technical work. At the design level, accessibility should be considered from the beginning of the project, not bolted on at the end. Retroactively adding screen reader support to a user interface that was not designed for it is far harder than building the user interface with screen reader support in mind from the start. Similarly, adding colorblind modes after all the art is finalized requires reworking assets, while designing the color palette with colorblindness in mind from the start requires only awareness and minor adjustments.

At the technical level, Bevy provides the building blocks for many accessibility features. Remappable controls are achievable through Bevy's input system, which provides abstract input actions that can be mapped to any physical input. Scalable text is achievable through Bevy's text rendering, which supports dynamic font sizes. Screen reader integration is a more complex challenge that requires platform-specific accessibility APIs (like the Accessibility API on macOS, UI Automation on Windows, and AT-SPI on Linux), and this is an area where Bevy's accessibility support is still developing. The Bevy community has expressed strong interest in built-in accessibility features, and contributions in this area are welcome and impactful.

Subtitle systems deserve special attention because they are one of the most commonly needed accessibility features and they are surprisingly nuanced to implement well. A good subtitle system needs to handle text wrapping, speaker identification (with consistent color coding or labels), timing (subtitles should appear slightly before the audio they represent and linger briefly after), positioning (subtitles should not cover important gameplay elements), background contrast (a semi-transparent background behind subtitle text dramatically improves readability), and customization (players should be able to adjust size, color, background opacity, and position). In Bevy, a subtitle system would typically be implemented as a user interface overlay that reads from a timed subtitle data structure (a list of text entries with start and end times) and manages the display of current subtitles based on the game clock or audio playback position.

The broader principle behind all of these features is that of universal design: designing for the widest range of users from the start, rather than designing for an "average" user and adding accommodations later. Universal design recognizes that there is no average user. Every player has a unique combination of abilities, preferences, and circumstances, and the more flexible and customizable a game is, the more players it can serve well.

Accessibility in games is not just a technical challenge or a moral imperative, though it is both of those things. It is also an opportunity. An opportunity to reach players who have been underserved by the industry, to demonstrate that inclusive design and compelling design are not in conflict, and to build games that more fully realize the medium's potential to bring joy, challenge, connection, and meaning to the broadest possible audience. Every accessibility feature you add is a door you open, and you may never know how many people walk through it, but they will be grateful that it was there.
