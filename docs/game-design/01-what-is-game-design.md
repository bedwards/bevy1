# What Game Design Is and What It Is Not

There is a persistent confusion in popular conversation about what game design actually means, and it tends to stem from the fact that the word "design" carries different weight depending on who is using it. When someone says they want to get into game design, they might mean they want to build games from scratch, writing code and wrangling physics engines and debugging shader pipelines until three in the morning. Or they might mean they want to paint concept art, model characters, or compose a sweeping orchestral score. All of those activities are part of making a game, but none of them, on their own, constitute game design. Game design is something both more specific and more elusive than any one discipline. It is the practice of deciding what a game asks of its players, how the game responds, and why any of that should matter to the person holding the controller, tapping the screen, or rolling the dice.

Think of it this way. A game developer is someone who builds a house. A game artist is someone who paints the walls and arranges the furniture. A game programmer is someone who installs the plumbing and wiring. A game designer is the person who decided the house should have three bedrooms, that the kitchen should open onto the living room so people can talk while they cook, and that there should be a window facing east so the morning light falls across the breakfast table. The designer is concerned with the experience of living in the house, not with the materials or techniques used to construct it. This is not to diminish those other roles, which are essential and deeply creative in their own right, but to clarify that game design is fundamentally about the structure of experience. It is about rules, choices, consequences, pacing, and the emotional arc that emerges when a human being sits down and engages with a system that has been crafted for their engagement.

This distinction matters more than it might seem, because conflating game design with game development leads people to misunderstand where the creative authority in a game actually lives. A beautifully rendered world with tight controls and flawless performance can still be a badly designed game if the choices it offers are meaningless, if its challenges are arbitrary, or if its systems fail to produce interesting situations. Conversely, a game with crude pixel art and simple mechanics can be brilliantly designed if every decision the player makes feels weighty, if the difficulty curve matches the learning curve, and if the systems interlock in ways that generate surprise and delight. Tetris is one of the best-designed games ever made, and it consists of falling blocks on a grid. The design is what makes it timeless, not the technology.

So what does a game designer actually do, day to day? They write design documents that describe how systems work and how they relate to each other. They prototype mechanics, often with paper and dice long before any code is written. They playtest relentlessly, watching people interact with their creation and noting where confusion sets in, where boredom creeps up, where frustration spikes, and where joy appears unexpectedly. They tune numbers, adjusting the speed of a character, the damage of a weapon, the frequency of a reward, the length of a cooldown timer, all in service of shaping how the game feels to play. They argue about whether a particular mechanic serves the game's core identity or distracts from it. They cut features they love because those features, however clever, do not serve the player's experience. The designer's job is to be the advocate for the player's experience at every stage of development, and this requires a strange combination of empathy, systems thinking, and willingness to let go of ideas that do not work, no matter how attached you are to them.

One of the most useful frameworks for thinking about game design was formalized in 2004 by Robin Hunicke, Marc LeBlanc, and Robert Zubek, three researchers who had been developing their ideas through workshops at the Game Developers Conference in San Jose. They called it the Mechanics, Dynamics, and Aesthetics framework, often abbreviated as MDA, and it provides a vocabulary for talking about games that separates three layers of experience that are often muddled together in casual conversation.


## The Mechanics, Dynamics, and Aesthetics Framework

The first layer of the MDA framework is mechanics. Mechanics are the concrete rules and systems that define what is possible within the game. They are the verbs a player can perform, the algorithms that govern the game world, the data structures that represent the state of play. In chess, the mechanics include the rules governing how each piece moves, the alternating turn structure, the conditions for check and checkmate, and the special rules like castling and en passant. In a first-person shooter, the mechanics include how the gun fires, how damage is calculated, how health regenerates, how the player moves through space. Mechanics are the building blocks, the atoms from which everything else is constructed, and they are the only layer of the game that the designer can directly control.

The second layer is dynamics, which describes what happens when mechanics interact with each other and with player behavior during actual play. Dynamics are the emergent patterns that arise from the rules. In chess, the mechanics say that a bishop moves diagonally and a rook moves in straight lines, but the dynamics include things like the concept of controlling the center of the board, the fork (a single piece threatening two enemy pieces simultaneously), and the long strategic arc of a positional game versus a tactical one. None of these things are written in the rules. They emerge from the rules when players interact with them. In a multiplayer shooter, the dynamics might include spawn camping, flanking strategies, the metagame that develops around which weapons players perceive as overpowered, and the social dynamics of team coordination. The designer does not create dynamics directly. The designer creates mechanics and hopes, through careful craft and extensive testing, that the dynamics will be interesting, varied, and satisfying.

The third layer is aesthetics, and here the word does not refer to visual beauty, as it often does in common usage, but to the emotional responses that the game evokes in the player. LeBlanc and his colleagues identified several categories of aesthetic experience that games can provide, including sensation (the pleasure of sensory stimuli), fantasy (the pleasure of inhabiting an imaginary role), narrative (the pleasure of unfolding drama), challenge (the pleasure of overcoming obstacles), fellowship (the pleasure of social connection), discovery (the pleasure of exploring and finding new things), expression (the pleasure of self-expression and creativity), and submission (the pleasure of entering a game as a pastime, a way to unwind). Most games aim to deliver a combination of these aesthetic experiences, and the designer's job is to understand which aesthetic goals the game is pursuing and to ensure that the mechanics produce dynamics that support those goals.

The crucial insight of the MDA framework is that designers and players experience games from opposite directions. The designer starts with mechanics, the concrete rules and systems they are building, and works outward through dynamics toward the aesthetic experience they hope to create. The player, however, encounters the game from the other direction entirely. The player first experiences the aesthetics, the feelings, the excitement, the curiosity, the tension, and only gradually comes to understand the dynamics that produce those feelings, and only if they dig deeper do they come to appreciate the underlying mechanics. This asymmetry has profound implications for how designers should think about their work. It means that getting the mechanics right is necessary but not sufficient. A mechanic that is logically elegant and mathematically balanced might still fail if it does not produce dynamics that the player finds emotionally engaging. The chain from mechanics to dynamics to aesthetics must be coherent and intentional at every link.

Consider a concrete example. Suppose you are designing a survival game and you want the player to feel a persistent sense of vulnerability and resourcefulness, an aesthetic goal that might be described as a mixture of challenge and fantasy. You decide to implement a hunger mechanic where the player must regularly find and consume food or suffer declining health. That is a mechanic, a concrete rule. But the dynamics that emerge from this mechanic depend on a hundred other design decisions. How scarce is food in the world? How quickly does hunger deplete? Can the player store food, or must they eat it immediately? Are there different types of food with different nutritional values? Can food spoil? Each of these decisions shapes the dynamics. If food is too abundant, the hunger mechanic becomes a minor annoyance, a pointless interruption that the player satisfies without thought, and the aesthetic goal of vulnerability is completely undermined. If food is too scarce, the game becomes an unrelenting grind where the player spends all their time foraging and never gets to engage with whatever else the game has to offer, and the aesthetic experience shifts from resourcefulness to tedium. The designer's task is to tune the mechanic so that the dynamics it produces serve the intended aesthetic, and this almost always requires extensive playtesting, because the relationship between mechanics and the experiences they generate is nonlinear and surprising, full of unintended consequences and counterintuitive interactions.


## The Role of the Designer as Advocate for the Player

This brings us to a philosophical point about what game design is at its core. Raph Koster, in his influential book "A Theory of Fun for Game Design," argues that games are fundamentally about pattern recognition and learning. When we play a game, we are learning to read its systems, to recognize its patterns, to develop strategies and heuristics for navigating its challenges. Fun, in Koster's framing, is the emotional reward the brain gives itself when it successfully learns something new. A game is fun when it presents a stream of learnable patterns at a pace that matches the player's ability to absorb them. A game becomes boring when the player has mastered all its patterns and there is nothing left to learn. A game becomes frustrating when the patterns are too complex, too opaque, or too random for the player to make sense of them. The designer's job, in this view, is to be a teacher of sorts, crafting a curriculum of challenges that unfolds at just the right pace to keep the player in a state of engaged learning.

Jesse Schell, in "The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses," takes a different but complementary approach. Schell argues that good game design comes from examining your game through many different perspectives, which he calls lenses. Each lens is a set of questions that illuminates a different aspect of the design. The Lens of Fun asks whether players are having fun, and if not, why not. The Lens of Surprise asks where the game's surprises come from and whether they are the right kind of surprises. The Lens of the Elemental Tetrad asks whether the game's mechanics, story, aesthetics, and technology are all working together in harmony. There are over a hundred of these lenses, and the point is not to apply all of them to every decision but to cultivate the habit of looking at your design from multiple angles, because the flaws in a design are often invisible from one perspective but glaringly obvious from another.

What both Koster and Schell share, along with the creators of the MDA framework, is the conviction that game design is a discipline with its own principles, its own body of knowledge, and its own modes of thinking. It is not just game development with a creative hat on. It is not just storytelling with interactivity bolted on. It is a practice that requires understanding human psychology, systems theory, probability, pacing, spatial reasoning, and the ineffable art of making something that feels good to interact with. It draws on all of these fields but is reducible to none of them.

The game design field in 2026 reflects this maturity. At the Game Developers Conference Festival of Gaming, held in March 2026 at the Moscone Center in San Francisco, over seven hundred sessions with more than a thousand speakers addressed every dimension of game creation, from the deeply technical to the broadly philosophical. The event created dedicated space for tabletop game design alongside digital game design, acknowledging that the principles of game design transcend any particular medium or technology. Whether you are designing a board game, a mobile puzzle, a massive multiplayer world, or an experimental interactive narrative, the core questions are the same. What choices does the player have? What are the consequences of those choices? How does the game communicate its rules and possibilities? What does it feel like to play? These are the questions that define game design as a discipline, and they are the questions that every game designer, whether working alone in a bedroom or leading a team of hundreds, must answer with intention, craft, and care.

One final point is worth making about what game design is not, because the negative space around the discipline helps define its shape. Game design is not the same as having ideas for games. Everyone has ideas for games, just as everyone has ideas for novels or films or restaurants. Ideas are plentiful and cheap. What distinguishes a game designer from someone with a game idea is the ability to take that idea and work through the thousand difficult decisions required to turn it into an experience that actually functions, that is playable and balanced and paced and tuned and tested and refined until it does what it is supposed to do, which is to create a particular kind of experience in the mind of a person who plays it. Game design is not a moment of inspiration. It is a long process of iteration, testing, and refinement, and the willingness to engage with that process, rather than the brilliance of any initial concept, is what separates the practice from the daydream.

Game design is also not the mere application of formulas or best practices. While frameworks like MDA and principles drawn from player psychology provide invaluable guidance, the actual work of design always involves judgment, intuition, and taste. Two designers can follow the same principles and arrive at radically different games, because the principles tell you what to think about, not what to decide. The designer must ultimately make choices that cannot be derived from any formula, choices about tone, about pacing, about what to include and what to leave out, about when to give the player freedom and when to constrain them, about when to challenge and when to reward. These are artistic decisions, and they are what make game design a creative discipline rather than an engineering problem. The frameworks and principles are tools in service of that creativity, and the best designers are those who have internalized those tools so thoroughly that they can apply them instinctively, the way a skilled writer applies the principles of grammar and rhetoric without consciously thinking about subject-verb agreement or the placement of a subordinate clause. The craft disappears into the art, and what remains is the experience of the player, which is, in the end, the only thing that matters.
