# Procedural Generation and the Spectrum of Authorship

Every game world is, in some sense, an act of authorship. Someone, or something, decided that the mountain should be here and the river should be there, that this corridor should turn left and that room should contain three enemies and a treasure chest. In traditionally authored games, a human designer makes every one of these decisions, placing every tree and stone and enemy with deliberate intent, crafting an experience as specific and curated as a novel or a film. In procedurally generated games, many or all of these decisions are delegated to algorithms, mathematical processes that produce content according to rules and constraints defined by the designer but whose specific outputs are not predetermined. The mountain is here because the noise function peaked at this coordinate. The corridor turns left because the graph-walk algorithm chose that branch. The room contains three enemies because the difficulty function evaluated to this value at this point in the dungeon.

Procedural generation is one of the oldest techniques in game design, stretching back to the earliest days of the medium. Rogue, the 1980 dungeon-crawling game that gave the roguelike genre its name, used procedural generation to create a new dungeon layout every time the player started a new game, ensuring that no two playthroughs were identical. The technique was born partly of necessity, because the storage limitations of early computers made it impractical to store large, hand-crafted worlds, and partly of ambition, because designers recognized that algorithmic generation could produce a kind of variety and replayability that no amount of hand-crafted content could match. If the dungeon is different every time, the player can never memorize its layout, can never develop a walkthrough, can never reduce the game to a solved problem. Every playthrough is a genuine exploration, and the sense of discovery remains fresh in a way that is impossible in a static world.

The techniques of procedural generation have grown enormously more sophisticated since the days of Rogue, and understanding the major approaches is useful both for designers who want to employ them and for those who want to understand the design trade-offs involved.


## Noise Functions and the Geometry of Randomness

The most widely used family of procedural generation techniques is based on noise functions, mathematical functions that produce values which appear random but which have a controlled spatial coherence, meaning that nearby points in space tend to have similar values. The most famous of these is Perlin noise, developed by Ken Perlin in 1983 while working on visual effects for the film Tron. Perlin was frustrated by the synthetic, obviously computer-generated look of the textures and surfaces being produced by existing techniques, and he wanted a way to generate randomness that looked natural, that had the organic variation of real-world surfaces without the harsh discontinuities and obvious repetition of simpler random functions.

The genius of Perlin noise lies in its ability to produce smooth, continuous randomness at multiple scales simultaneously. A single layer of Perlin noise produces gently rolling hills of random values, like a landscape seen from very high up. By combining multiple layers at different frequencies and amplitudes, a technique called fractal noise or fractional Brownian motion, the designer can produce patterns that have both large-scale structure and fine-grained detail, much like a real landscape that has both mountain ranges and individual boulders. This technique is used to generate terrain heightmaps, where the noise value at each point determines the elevation of the ground, producing landscapes that are convincingly natural-looking despite being entirely synthetic. It is also used to generate textures, clouds, vegetation distribution, cave systems, and a vast array of other natural-looking phenomena.

The power of noise-based generation is that it is continuous, meaning it can provide a value for any point in space without needing to reference a grid or a database. This makes it ideal for generating infinite worlds, because the algorithm can generate terrain on the fly for any coordinate the player visits, without needing to precompute or store the entire world. Minecraft, perhaps the most famous procedurally generated game of all time, uses noise functions to generate its virtually infinite landscapes of blocks, with different noise layers controlling the base terrain shape, the biome distribution, the cave networks, and the placement of ores and other underground features.

But noise-based generation has significant limitations. Because noise functions are fundamentally about smooth, continuous variation, they are poorly suited to generating content that has discrete, structured properties, things like buildings, rooms, roads, or any other content where the relationships between elements matter more than the values at individual points. You can use Perlin noise to generate a beautiful mountain range, but you cannot use it to generate a city, because a city is not a smooth function of space. A city is a network of relationships: streets connect to other streets, buildings face streets, rooms connect to hallways, doors are placed in walls at appropriate locations. This kind of structural content requires different algorithms.


## Wave Function Collapse and the Logic of Constraint

One of the most exciting developments in procedural generation in recent years is the wave function collapse algorithm, created by Maxim Gumin and inspired, as its name suggests, by concepts from quantum mechanics. The algorithm works by a process of constraint propagation that is conceptually similar to solving a Sudoku puzzle. The designer provides a set of tiles, small pieces of content that can be placed adjacent to each other, along with rules specifying which tiles can be neighbors. The algorithm then fills a grid with tiles, one at a time, choosing each tile randomly from the set of tiles that are consistent with the constraints imposed by its already-placed neighbors, and propagating the consequences of each placement to update the possibilities for nearby cells.

The result is content that is locally coherent, because every tile is compatible with its neighbors, and globally varied, because the random choices made during generation produce different overall patterns each time. Wave function collapse is particularly effective at generating content that has a tiled or modular structure: dungeon layouts, cityscapes, textile patterns, circuit board designs, and any other content where the challenge is not to generate smooth continuous values but to assemble discrete pieces in a way that satisfies a set of relational constraints.

What makes wave function collapse particularly appealing to designers is that it preserves artistic intent far more than noise-based generation. The tiles themselves are hand-crafted, designed by an artist or level designer to have specific visual and functional properties. The algorithm does not create new content from scratch; it arranges existing content according to rules that the designer defines. This means that the output of wave function collapse has a curated quality, a coherence and intentionality, that is difficult to achieve with noise-based methods. The designer controls the vocabulary, the set of tiles, and the grammar, the adjacency rules, and the algorithm writes the sentences. The result feels authored even though no human specified the exact arrangement.

The comparison between Perlin noise and wave function collapse illustrates a fundamental trade-off in procedural generation. Noise-based methods give the designer control over the statistical properties of the output, its smoothness, its frequency, its amplitude, but not over its structural properties. Wave function collapse gives the designer control over the structural properties, the types of elements and the relationships between them, but the statistical properties of the output emerge from the interaction of the constraints rather than being directly specified. In practice, many modern games use both techniques in combination: noise functions for large-scale terrain and environmental features, wave function collapse or similar constraint-based algorithms for structures and layouts, with additional hand-crafted content layered on top for key narrative or gameplay moments. This hybrid approach allows the designer to benefit from the strengths of each technique while mitigating their limitations.


## The Design Philosophy of Procedural Generation

The technical details of procedural generation algorithms are important, but the more fundamental question for the game designer is not "how does the algorithm work?" but "when should I use procedural generation, and when should I not?" This is a design question rather than a technical one, and the answer depends on what kind of experience you are trying to create.

Procedural generation excels at producing variety and replayability. If your game is designed around repeated playthroughs, each of which should feel fresh, procedural generation is almost certainly part of the answer. The roguelike genre, which has experienced a renaissance in recent years with games like Hades, Slay the Spire, and their many descendants, is built on this principle. Each run through the game presents a different configuration of rooms, enemies, items, and challenges, forcing the player to adapt their strategy to the specific circumstances they encounter rather than memorizing a fixed sequence. The replayability comes not from the content itself, which is typically drawn from a finite pool, but from the combinatorial variety of configurations, which can be astronomical even with a relatively small set of elements.

Procedural generation also excels at producing scale. If your game requires a world that is too large to author by hand, either because the world is literally infinite, as in Minecraft, or because the volume of content required exceeds what a human team can produce, procedural generation can fill in the gaps. But this is where a crucial caveat enters: procedurally generated content, even content produced by sophisticated algorithms, tends to lack the intentionality and specificity of hand-crafted content. A procedurally generated dungeon will have rooms and corridors and enemies placed according to rules, but it will not have the narrative coherence, the environmental storytelling, the carefully paced emotional arc that a human designer can create. A procedurally generated landscape will have mountains and rivers and forests, but it will not have the deliberate composition, the meaningful vistas, the designed sightlines that a human artist can provide.

This is not a failing of the algorithms; it is a fundamental property of the approach. Procedural generation produces content by following rules, and rules can encode constraints and preferences and statistical properties, but they cannot encode meaning. A human designer places a ruined bridge not because a noise function peaked at that location but because the ruined bridge tells a story, because it creates a moment of emotional resonance, because it serves a narrative purpose that is connected to the game's themes and the player's journey. No algorithm currently knows how to make decisions for those reasons, because those reasons are rooted in human understanding of narrative, emotion, and significance.

This means that the most effective use of procedural generation is typically in combination with hand-crafted content rather than as a replacement for it. The procedural systems handle the parts of the game that benefit from variety and scale, the dungeon layouts, the terrain, the distribution of common encounters, while the hand-crafted content handles the parts that benefit from intentionality and meaning, the boss encounters, the key narrative moments, the set-piece levels that showcase the game's best ideas. Hades exemplifies this approach beautifully: the room-to-room progression is procedurally generated, creating a different sequence each run, but the individual rooms, the characters, the story beats, and the boss encounters are all hand-crafted, ensuring that the game's narrative and emotional core remains strong regardless of the procedural variation that surrounds it.

There is also a middle ground between pure procedural generation and pure hand-crafting that is worth exploring. Designers can create systems where the procedural generation produces a rough draft that is then refined by hand, or where the hand-crafted content is arranged procedurally within a larger structure. They can create templates or archetypes that constrain the procedural generation, ensuring that the output always has certain properties, always contains certain elements, always follows certain patterns, while still varying in the specifics. This templated approach combines the variety of procedural generation with the intentionality of hand-crafting, and it is the approach used by many of the most successful procedurally generated games.

The decision of whether and how to use procedural generation is, like all design decisions, ultimately a question of what serves the player's experience. If the game benefits from surprise and adaptation, if the player's engagement comes from responding to novel situations rather than mastering a fixed challenge, if the game's systems are deep enough that different configurations produce genuinely different strategic landscapes, then procedural generation has much to offer. If the game's strength lies in authored moments, in carefully crafted encounters and narratively meaningful spaces, in the specific rather than the variable, then procedural generation may introduce noise where the game needs signal, variety where it needs specificity, randomness where it needs intention. The wise designer understands both the power and the limitations of procedural generation and uses it where it serves the game, not where it merely fills space.

There is a final observation worth making about procedural generation that connects it to the broader themes of game design. The appeal of procedural generation, both for designers and for players, is rooted in the same psychological principle that drives much of what makes games engaging: the desire for discovery. A procedurally generated world is a world that the designer themselves has not fully explored, a world that holds genuine surprises even for its creator. This quality gives procedurally generated games a sense of vastness and possibility that is difficult to replicate in authored content, a feeling that the world extends beyond what anyone has seen, that there are mountains no one has climbed and caves no one has explored and configurations no one has encountered. Whether this vastness is meaningful or merely large depends on the quality of the systems that generate it and the care with which those systems have been designed, but when it works, procedural generation produces something that no amount of hand-crafting can achieve: a world that is genuinely unknown, even to the people who made it, and that rewards exploration with the only thing that exploration truly demands, which is the possibility of finding something that no one has found before.
