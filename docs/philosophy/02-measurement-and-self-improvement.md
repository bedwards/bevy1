# Measuring What Matters: Empirical Self-Improvement in Software Development

The instinct to measure is one of the oldest in engineering, far older than software itself. A carpenter measures twice and cuts once not because measuring is inherently enjoyable but because the cost of cutting wrong is high and the cost of measuring is low. Software development has a peculiar relationship with measurement, though, because the things that matter most — code quality, developer productivity, architectural soundness, the likelihood that a system will still be maintainable in five years — are notoriously difficult to quantify. Lines of code tell you almost nothing useful. Code coverage percentages can be gamed to meaninglessness. Velocity points in agile methodologies measure effort estimates rather than value delivered. And yet the desire to measure persists, because without measurement you are navigating by intuition alone, and intuition is a wonderful servant but a terrible compass when conditions change.

The challenge for a project like ours, which sits at the intersection of game development, open-source tooling, and AI-assisted workflows, is to identify measurements that are both meaningful and actionable. A meaningful measurement tells you something true about the state of your system. An actionable measurement tells you something you can respond to — something that, when it moves in the wrong direction, suggests a specific intervention, and when it moves in the right direction, confirms that your current approach is working. The combination of these two properties is rarer than you might expect, and finding the right measurements for your specific context is itself a skill worth developing deliberately.

Let us start with the most concrete layer: the performance of the code itself. In a game engine context, the frame time — how many milliseconds elapse between one rendered frame and the next — is the king of all metrics. Players experience frame time directly, as smoothness or stutter, as responsiveness or lag. A game that consistently renders in under sixteen milliseconds delivers sixty frames per second, which most players perceive as smooth. A game that occasionally spikes to fifty milliseconds will feel jerky, even if the average frame time is excellent, because human perception is disproportionately sensitive to outliers. This means that measuring average frame time is insufficient; you need to measure percentiles, particularly the ninety-ninth percentile and the ninety-ninth point nine percentile, because these outliers are what players actually feel.

Bevy provides built-in diagnostics for frame time, entity count, and system execution time, and these diagnostics deserve a permanent place in your development workflow, not as numbers you check occasionally but as instruments you glance at constantly, the way a pilot glances at the altimeter. When you add a new system, does the frame time budget change? When you spawn a thousand more entities, does the entity iteration time scale linearly or worse? These are empirical questions with empirical answers, and the only way to answer them is to measure, not to guess. A common trap in game development is to assume that a particular optimization is necessary or unnecessary based on intuition, when five minutes of profiling would reveal the truth. The profiler does not lie, and it does not have preconceptions about which code paths are fast or slow.

Beyond frame time, there are system-level measurements that matter for a Bevy application. How long does each schedule take to execute? Which systems consume the most time? Are there systems that rarely do meaningful work but still occupy a slot in the scheduler? Bevy's parallel scheduler is remarkably efficient, but it can only parallelize systems whose data access does not conflict, and it cannot make a fundamentally expensive system cheaper. If your movement system iterates over every entity in the world but only ten of them actually need to move, that is a measurement that suggests a design improvement — perhaps a marker component that limits the query to only the entities that matter, or a spatial partitioning structure that reduces the search space.

The next layer of measurement concerns the development process itself, not the runtime behavior of the code but the efficiency and reliability of the workflow that produces it. How often does the continuous integration pipeline catch errors? If it catches errors frequently, that could mean two things: either the CI is doing its job well, which is good, or the code being submitted is of poor quality, which warrants investigation. How long does the CI pipeline take to run? If a full pipeline takes twenty minutes, developers will batch their changes and submit less frequently, which means larger, harder-to-review pull requests and more merge conflicts. If the pipeline runs in under three minutes, developers will submit frequently and get fast feedback, which leads to smaller, cleaner changes. The speed of your feedback loop is itself a thing worth measuring and optimizing, because it has a multiplicative effect on every other aspect of productivity.

When working with an LLM as a development partner, a new category of measurements becomes relevant: how often does the generated code pass the deterministic verification layer on the first attempt? If the LLM generates code that compiles and passes tests eighty percent of the time, that tells you something about the quality of your prompts, the clarity of your project conventions, and the difficulty of the task at hand. If that number drops to forty percent for a particular category of task, that suggests either that the task is genuinely hard, that the LLM lacks sufficient context, or that your verification layer has become more stringent — all of which are useful signals. Over time, tracking this acceptance rate lets you calibrate your workflow: you learn which tasks to delegate confidently and which to supervise more closely, which prompting strategies work and which waste time, and where your test suite has gaps that allow incorrect code to slip through.

There is a philosophical dimension to measurement that is worth acknowledging honestly. Every measurement system creates incentives, and those incentives shape behavior in ways that can be subtle and counterproductive. If you measure lines of code, people write more lines. If you measure the number of tests, people write trivial tests. If you measure code coverage, people write tests that exercise code paths without actually verifying behavior. The phenomenon is well known enough to have a name — Goodhart's Law, which states roughly that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The antidote to Goodhart's Law is not to stop measuring but to measure multiple things simultaneously, to change what you measure periodically, and to always treat measurements as information rather than goals. A frame time measurement that drifts upward is information that something has gotten slower. The goal is not to make the number go down — the goal is to understand why it went up and whether that tradeoff is acceptable.

This brings us to the practice of continuous self-improvement, which is the art of using measurements not as report cards but as navigational instruments. The essence of continuous improvement is a cycle: observe the current state, form a hypothesis about what could be better, make a change, measure the result, and decide whether to keep the change, revert it, or refine it further. This cycle operates at multiple timescales. Within a single coding session, you might notice that a particular system is slow, profile it, find a bottleneck, optimize it, and measure the improvement — all within an hour. Across a sprint, you might notice that code reviews are taking too long, experiment with smaller pull requests, and measure whether the review turnaround improves. Across a quarter, you might notice that certain categories of bugs keep recurring, invest in better testing infrastructure for those categories, and measure whether the recurrence rate drops.

The key insight is that each of these cycles generates empirical evidence, and empirical evidence is qualitatively different from opinion or intuition. When someone says "I think our code is getting slower," that is an opinion. When the profiler shows that the ninety-ninth percentile frame time has increased from twelve to eighteen milliseconds over the past month, that is evidence. When someone says "the LLM is not helpful for UI work," that is an opinion. When your logs show that LLM-generated UI code passes CI on the first try only thirty percent of the time, compared to seventy percent for game logic code, that is evidence. Evidence does not replace judgment — you still need to decide what to do about it — but it grounds judgment in reality rather than letting it float free in the space of vague impressions.

For a Bevy game project specifically, there are several categories of measurement that deserve ongoing attention. Build time is one: Rust's compilation model means that full builds can be slow, and Bevy is a large dependency, so incremental build time matters enormously for developer experience. Tracking how long it takes to compile after a single-file change tells you whether your workspace structure and caching strategy are effective. Entity count and component storage efficiency matter as your game world grows: if you find that you have entities with twenty components, that may indicate a design problem where entities are accumulating responsibilities that should be split across multiple entities. System execution time, measured per-system rather than in aggregate, tells you where optimization effort would be most rewarded.

At the process level, tracking the time from "code written" to "code merged" reveals bottlenecks in your workflow. If code sits waiting for CI for ten minutes, maybe your pipeline needs parallelization. If code sits waiting for review for two days, maybe your team needs better review habits or smaller pull requests. If code frequently fails CI after being marked ready, maybe your local development environment does not match CI closely enough, and you should invest in making them more similar, perhaps with pre-commit hooks or a local CI runner.

The deepest form of measurement, and the hardest to get right, concerns the quality of the software as experienced by its users. For a game, this means playtesting — not automated testing, which verifies that the code does what the developer intended, but human testing, which verifies that what the developer intended is actually fun, intuitive, and engaging. Playtesting generates qualitative data that resists easy quantification, but it can be structured to produce useful patterns: if three out of five playtesters fail to discover a core mechanic, that is a design problem, not a testing problem. If every playtester complains about input latency, that is a measurement you can trace back to frame time or input processing order. The connection between subjective experience and objective measurement is the bridge that connects engineering to craft, and maintaining that bridge requires ongoing attention, humility, and a willingness to let evidence override ego.

In the end, the practice of measurement and self-improvement is really a practice of intellectual honesty. It means admitting that you do not know whether your code is fast until you profile it, that you do not know whether your process is efficient until you time it, and that you do not know whether your game is fun until someone plays it. These admissions are not signs of weakness — they are the precondition for genuine improvement. The team that measures, reflects, and adjusts will always outperform the team that assumes, defends, and continues, because the former is navigating by instruments while the latter is navigating by faith. And while faith has its place in many domains of human life, software engineering is not one of them.
