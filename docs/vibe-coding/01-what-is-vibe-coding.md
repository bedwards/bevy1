# What Is Vibe Coding

On February 2, 2025, Andrej Karpathy, a computer scientist who co-founded OpenAI and led the artificial intelligence division at Tesla, posted a short message on X describing something he called vibe coding. "There's a new kind of coding I call vibe coding," he wrote, "where you fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists." He described a workflow in which he spoke to an AI assistant through a voice interface, accepted every change the model proposed without reading the diffs, and when an error appeared, simply copied the stack trace back into the chat and let the model try again. The phrase caught fire almost immediately. Merriam-Webster listed it as a slang and trending expression within weeks, and Collins English Dictionary named it the Word of the Year for 2025. By the time academic workshops and university press offices started publishing explainers in early 2026, vibe coding had already become the dominant shorthand for the entire phenomenon of building software by describing what you want in plain language and letting a large language model write the implementation.

But the popularity of the term has created a problem, because vibe coding in the strict sense that Karpathy described it, the sense where you do not even look at the code, is only one point along a much wider spectrum. Simon Willison, a respected voice in the developer community, has argued that applying the label to all forms of AI-assisted programming dilutes its meaning and gives outsiders a misleading impression of what responsible practitioners actually do. In Willison's framing, if a large language model wrote every line of your code but you reviewed it, tested it, and understood it, that is not vibe coding at all; that is using the model as a very fast typing assistant. He proposed a separate term, vibe engineering, to describe the work of seasoned professionals who accelerate their output with AI while remaining fully accountable for what they ship. The distinction matters because the risks, the workflows, and the verification requirements differ enormously depending on where you sit along that spectrum, and conflating the endpoints makes it harder to think clearly about any of them.

## The Spectrum from Manual to Autonomous

Imagine a continuum. At the far left sits traditional software development, where a human writes every character, reasons about every branch, and takes full responsibility for correctness, performance, security, and maintainability. Move a step to the right and you find tab-completion tools like GitHub Copilot, which suggest the next few tokens or lines based on the surrounding context, and the developer accepts, rejects, or edits each suggestion in real time. The developer remains firmly in charge; the tool merely reduces keystrokes. Move further right and you encounter agentic coding assistants like Claude Code, Cursor Composer, or Aider, where you describe a task in natural language and the agent reads multiple files, proposes edits across the codebase, runs commands, observes outputs, and iterates until the task appears complete. Here the developer is reviewing completed work rather than steering each line, which is a meaningful shift in cognitive mode but not necessarily a relinquishment of understanding. Move further still and you reach the zone Karpathy described, where the developer does not review the generated code at all, trusting the model's output on faith and treating errors as signals to feed back into the loop rather than as things to diagnose personally. And at the very far right of the spectrum sit fully autonomous agents that receive a specification, spin up isolated environments, write code, run tests, fix failures, open pull requests, and never involve a human until the work is done or the agent gets stuck.

Each position on this continuum involves a different balance of speed against understanding, and a different set of failure modes. The further right you go, the faster you can produce artifacts, but the less likely you are to catch subtle bugs, security vulnerabilities, architectural mismatches, or accumulated technical debt that will slow you down weeks or months later. Anthropic's own research underscores this trade-off. In a randomized controlled trial published in early 2026, researchers asked fifty-two mostly junior engineers to learn an unfamiliar asynchronous programming library. Half the group used an AI coding assistant and half coded manually. The AI group finished tasks slightly faster, but on a subsequent quiz measuring understanding, they scored an average of fifty percent compared to sixty-seven percent for the manual group, a gap equivalent to nearly two letter grades. The largest deficit appeared in debugging questions, the very skill that matters most when something goes wrong in production and no model can help you because the problem is too novel, too entangled, or too context-dependent for a prompt to capture. Crucially, however, the study also found that developers who used the AI for conceptual inquiry, asking it to explain library design decisions or verify their understanding of concurrency patterns, scored sixty-five percent or higher, nearly matching the manual group. The way you use the tool changes what you learn, and what you learn determines whether you can maintain and extend the code the tool helped you write.

## Where Vibe Coding Works and Where It Does Not

Given those dynamics, vibe coding in the strict, diffs-unread, vibes-only sense works best in situations where the cost of failure is low and the value of speed is high. Building a weekend prototype to test a product hypothesis, throwing together a personal utility script, constructing a one-off data visualization for a blog post, assembling an internal tool that three people will use for two months and then discard: these are contexts where the downside of a subtle bug is measured in minutes of annoyance rather than breached customer data or corrupted financial records. The vibe coder can move extraordinarily fast in these settings, producing in an afternoon what might have taken a week of careful manual work, and the trade-off is rational because the thing being built does not need to be robust, secure, or maintainable over a long time horizon.

The trouble arrives when people generalize from those successes to high-stakes contexts. A prototype that impresses stakeholders gets promoted to production. An internal tool that was supposed to serve three people somehow serves three hundred. A solo developer who vibed their way to a minimum viable product now needs to onboard collaborators who cannot understand the code because nobody ever understood it, including the person who shipped it. These failure modes are not hypothetical; they are the predictable consequence of a workflow that optimizes for generation speed at the expense of comprehension. The New Stack ran a piece in early 2026 warning that unreviewed AI-generated code in production could cause "catastrophic explosions," and while that language is deliberately dramatic, the underlying concern is legitimate: code that no human has read is code that no human can debug, audit, or reason about when something unexpected happens.

Apple's decision in March 2026 to push back on App Store submissions from "vibe coding" apps provides a concrete institutional signal about where the practice bumps up against real-world quality expectations. Although Apple later clarified it does not have rules specifically targeting AI-generated code, the initial enforcement actions reflected a pattern reviewers were seeing: apps built with minimal human oversight that crashed frequently, handled user data carelessly, or provided a degraded experience compared to apps whose developers had actually understood and tested their code. The lesson is not that AI-generated code is inherently inferior; it is that code which has not been reviewed by a competent human is unreliable regardless of who or what wrote it, and the speed advantage of vibe coding vanishes if you spend that saved time dealing with rejections, regressions, and customer complaints.

## The Importance of Verification Layers

The most productive practitioners in 2026 are neither pure vibe coders nor purists who refuse to let a model touch their repositories. They occupy the middle of the spectrum, using AI to draft code rapidly while maintaining robust verification systems that catch mistakes before those mistakes reach users. The term that has emerged for this disciplined middle ground is structured AI-first development, and its defining characteristic is not the use of AI per se but the presence of verification layers that compensate for the fact that generated code has not been manually reasoned about line by line.

What does a verification layer look like in practice? It starts with tests, and this is one area where AI assistance is almost unambiguously positive. A developer who writes tests first, or who asks the AI to generate tests alongside the implementation, creates a safety net that constrains the space of acceptable outputs. If the model produces code that passes the tests and the tests are themselves well-conceived, the code is very likely correct for the scenarios those tests cover, even if no human read the implementation. Test-driven vibe development, as some practitioners have started calling it, inverts the traditional vibe coding workflow by generating tests from natural-language acceptance criteria before generating the functional code, so the AI is writing to a specification rather than freestyling against an ambiguous prompt. The result is faster than pure manual development but far more reliable than unverified generation, because the tests serve as a machine-readable statement of intent that the model must satisfy.

Beyond tests, verification layers include static analysis tools, linters, type checkers, and security scanners that can catch entire categories of defects automatically. They include code review, whether by a human colleague or by a separate AI model acting as a critic rather than a generator. They include architectural review, where a senior developer examines not the line-level implementation but the structural decisions, the module boundaries, the dependency choices, the data flow patterns, to ensure the generated code fits coherently into the larger system. And they include monitoring and observability in production, so that even if a defect slips through all the pre-deployment gates, you learn about it quickly and can trace it to its source.

The key insight is that vibe coding is not a binary choice between writing everything yourself and trusting the machine absolutely. It is a dial you can turn, and the appropriate setting depends on the stakes involved, the maturity of your verification infrastructure, and your own depth of understanding of the domain. A senior engineer with excellent tests, a strong linter, and deep familiarity with the problem domain can afford to turn the dial further toward the AI-generated end of the spectrum because their verification layers will catch most of what goes wrong and their domain knowledge will help them notice the rest. A junior engineer working on an unfamiliar codebase in a security-sensitive domain should keep the dial closer to the manual end, using the AI for explanation and exploration rather than wholesale code generation, because they do not yet have the mental models needed to recognize when the AI has made a plausible-looking but fundamentally wrong decision.

The maturation of vibe coding from Karpathy's playful tweet to the structured, layered, verification-rich discipline it is becoming in 2026 mirrors the maturation of every other powerful technology that arrived faster than the practices around it. The automobile preceded traffic laws, seatbelts, and driver's licenses by decades. The internet preceded encryption standards, content moderation, and privacy regulations by just as long. What we are living through now is the period in which the community figures out the norms, the safeguards, and the institutional structures that will let this astonishingly productive technology deliver its benefits without its most easily avoidable harms. The developers who thrive will be the ones who embrace the speed and generative power of AI while insisting, rigorously and without compromise, on verification at every layer of the stack. They will vibe when vibing is appropriate, and they will read every line when reading every line is what the situation demands. The vibes, it turns out, are only as good as the verification that follows them.
